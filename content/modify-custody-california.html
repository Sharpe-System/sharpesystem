When you can modify custody

In general, the court can modify custody and visitation orders when a change is in a child’s best interests. The specific burden can depend on what type of order you have now and what change you’re requesting.

Common “change” triggers

  • Schedule is unworkable (school, activities, distance, work changes)
  • Repeated missed exchanges or chronic interference with parenting time
  • Safety concerns (substance abuse, violence, dangerous supervision)
  • Child’s needs changed (education, medical, developmental)
  • Relocation or frequent travel affecting stability
  • Noncompliance with existing orders (pattern, not one-off)

What courts dislike

  • Vague requests (“I want more time”) without a precise schedule
  • Evidence-free accusations or emotional narratives
  • “Gotcha” filings meant to punish the other parent
  • Proposals that ignore school logistics and exchange feasibility
  • Non-specific “safety” claims with no dates, details, or sources

Evidence courts look for

The court does not decide custody based on who tells the most dramatic story. It responds to organized facts with dates, consistency, and practical solutions.

High-value evidence types

  • Communication logs showing requests, responses, delays, and refusals (time-stamped)
  • School and activity records (attendance, communications, schedules)
  • Medical records or provider statements (when relevant and lawful)
  • Police reports (if applicable; courts weight them differently than convictions)
  • Third-party documentation (providers, coaches, tutors) where appropriate
  • Calendar timelines that show patterns (not single events)

Steps to file (high level)

  1. Define the exact schedule you want (days, exchange times/locations, holidays).
  2. Write the reason as a structured pattern (what changed, when, what impact).
  3. Attach only relevant exhibits (organized, labeled, referenced in your narrative).
  4. File and serve correctly (court rules vary; follow your county’s requirements).
  5. Prepare for hearing with a clean timeline and short, factual talking points.

Common mistakes

Drafting mistakes

  • Mixing legal requests with personal grievances
  • Not proposing a complete schedule (court can’t “guess” for you)
  • Overloading exhibits (quantity ≠ credibility)
  • Failing to connect each request to child-centered outcomes

Process mistakes

  • Waiting until the last second (service and hearing prep take time)
  • Using the child as messenger
  • Communicating off-platform when your case requires a documented channel
  • Showing up without a clean timeline and exhibit references
